Censorship in George Orwell's *1984* and Its Modern Relevance

Censorship is a pervasive and complex issue that plays a central role in George Orwell’s *1984*. In the novel, the Party—led by the figurehead of Big Brother—employs censorship as a primary tool to control the populace and maintain its totalitarian regime. Orwell’s exploration of censorship in *1984* demonstrates how control over information, manipulation of language, and the suppression of dissent create an oppressive society. The novel's themes remain relevant today, as governments and institutions continue to wield similar tactics in the real world.

1. Control of Information

  • Ministry of Truth: The ministry’s name is deeply ironic, as its function is to constantly rewrite history and fabricate facts to align with the Party’s needs.
  • Newspeak: The Party introduces Newspeak, a language designed to eliminate words that could express dissenting ideas.
  • Unperson: Those who fall out of favor with the Party are erased not only from the present but also from history.

2. Psychological Manipulation

  • Doublethink: The ability to hold two contradictory beliefs simultaneously.
  • Fear and Surveillance: The ever-present fear of surveillance reinforces self-censorship.
  • Cult of Personality: Big Brother is elevated to an almost god-like status, with loyalty demanded from all citizens.

3. Historical Revisionism

  • The Past is Changeable: By constantly rewriting history, the Party can justify its present actions.
  • "Who controls the past controls the future. Who controls the present controls the past.": This quote encapsulates the Party’s strategy.
